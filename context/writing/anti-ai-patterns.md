# Anti-AI Writing Patterns
The universal signs of AI-generated writing to avoid.

Based on the Wikipedia field guide to detecting AI-generated content. Use this as a checklist of patterns to actively prevent in all output.

---

## The Core Problem

LLMs use statistical algorithms to predict what comes next. This causes regression to the mean: specific, unusual, nuanced facts get replaced with generic, positive-sounding language. The subject becomes simultaneously less specific and more exaggerated. Like a portrait fading from a sharp photograph into a blurry, generic sketch.

---

## Content Patterns to Avoid

### 1. Undue Emphasis on Significance, Legacy, and Broader Trends

**Words to watch:** stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role/moment, underscores/highlights its importance/significance, reflects broader, symbolizing its ongoing/enduring/lasting, contributing to the, setting the stage for, marking/shaping the, represents/marks a shift, key turning point, evolving landscape, focal point, indelible mark, deeply rooted

LLMs puff up the importance of subject matter by adding statements about how arbitrary aspects represent or contribute to broader topics. They do this even for mundane subjects. Sometimes they acknowledge the subject is unimportant, then talk about its importance anyway.

### 2. Superficial Analyses

**Words to watch:** highlighting/underscoring/emphasizing..., ensuring..., reflecting/symbolizing..., contributing to..., cultivating/fostering... (figurative), encompassing..., valuable insights, align/resonate with

AI inserts shallow analysis of information, often via present participle ("-ing") phrases tacked onto the end of sentences. These add no real insight. Example: "These names highlight the collaborative nature of mosque construction and emphasize the contributions of skilled artisans."

### 3. Promotional and Ad-Like Language

**Words to watch:** boasts a, vibrant, rich (figurative), profound, enhancing its, showcasing, exemplifies, commitment to, natural beauty, nestled, in the heart of, groundbreaking (figurative), renowned

LLMs struggle to maintain neutral tone. They insert promotional language even when prompted to be encyclopedic. Anything touching "cultural heritage" gets constant reminders of its importance.

### 4. Vague Attributions and Overgeneralization

**Words to watch:** Industry reports, Observers have cited, Experts argue, Some critics argue, several sources/publications (when only few cited), such as (before exhaustive lists)

AI attributes opinions to vague authorities. It exaggerates the quantity of sources backing a claim. Presents one or two sources as "widely held" views.

### 5. Outline-Like Conclusions About Challenges

**Words to watch:** Despite its... faces several challenges..., Despite these challenges, Challenges and Legacy, Future Outlook

A rigid formula: positive assessment, then "Despite its [positive words], [subject] faces challenges including..." ending with vague optimism about future initiatives. This pattern is about the rigid formula, not simply mentioning challenges.

## Language and Grammar Patterns to Avoid

### 6. Overused AI Vocabulary

**High-frequency AI words:** Additionally (especially starting sentences), align with, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight (as verb), interplay, intricate/intricacies, key (as adjective), landscape (abstract), pivotal, showcase, tapestry (abstract), testament, underscore (as verb), valuable, vibrant

These words started appearing far more frequently in text after 2023. One or two may be coincidental. An edit introducing many of them, many times, is one of the strongest tells.

### 7. Avoidance of Basic Copulatives

**Words to watch:** serves as/stands as/marks/represents [a], boasts/features/offers [a]

AI substitutes "serves as a" or "marks the" for simpler "is" or "are." Prefers "features," "offers" over "has." Sometimes more elaborate: "ventured into politics as a candidate" instead of "was a candidate."

### 8. Negative Parallelisms

Parallel constructions with "not/but/however": "Not only... but...", "It is not just about..., it's..." Used to appear balanced and thoughtful. Also: "not..., it's..." or "no..., no..., just..." constructions that explicitly negate primary properties.

### 9. Rule of Three

AI overuses the rule of three: "adjective, adjective, adjective" or "short phrase, short phrase, and short phrase." Often used to make shallow analyses appear comprehensive.

### 10. Elegant Variation

AI has a repetition-penalty, causing it to swap synonyms unnecessarily. A character's name becomes "the protagonist," then "the key player," then "the eponymous character." Avoid cycling through synonyms when repeating the actual term is clearer.

### 11. False Ranges

AI uses meaningless "from... to..." constructions where the endpoints are loosely related or unrelated and no meaningful scale exists. Example: "from problem-solving and tool-making to scientific discovery, artistic expression, and technological innovation." These mimic persuasive writing but convey nothing.

## Formatting Patterns to Avoid

### 12. Title Case in Headings

AI strongly capitalizes all main words in section headings. Use sentence case instead.

### 13. Overuse of Boldface

AI bolds every instance of a chosen term or phrase in a "key takeaways" fashion. Also creates vertical lists where every item starts with an inline boldfaced header followed by a colon. Use bold sparingly for genuine emphasis.

### 14. Overuse of Em Dashes

AI uses em dashes more often than human writers, in formulaic ways that mimic "punched up" sales writing. Often placed where commas, parentheses, or colons would be more natural.

### 15. Emoji Decoration

AI decorates section headings or bullet points with emoji. Do not use emoji in professional/educational content.

---

## Structural Patterns to Avoid

### 16. Didactic Disclaimers

"It's important to note/remember/consider..." These safety-net phrases add nothing. Either the information matters (include it) or it does not (cut it).

### 17. Section Summaries and Conclusions

"In summary," "In conclusion," "Overall" -- followed by restating the section's core idea. Cut these entirely. The reader just read the section.

### 18. Collaborative Communication Leakage

"I hope this helps," "Of course!", "Certainly!", "Would you like...", "let me know," "here is a..." -- these are chatbot interface phrases that should never appear in finished content.

### 19. Knowledge-Cutoff Disclaimers

"As of [date]," "While specific details are limited/scarce...," "not widely available/documented" -- AI inserts these when it lacks information. Often paired with speculation about what the information "likely" is.

## The Master Checklist

Before publishing anything, scan for:

1. **Puffery:** Does the text inflate the importance of mundane details?
2. **Vagueness:** Are claims attributed to unnamed "experts" or "observers"?
3. **AI vocabulary cluster:** Count instances of the flagged words. More than 3-4 in a short piece is a red flag.
4. **Shallow analysis via "-ing" phrases:** Are participle phrases doing analytical work they cannot actually do?
5. **Promotional tone:** Would this sound at home in a press release?
6. **Formulaic structure:** Does it follow the "positive assessment, challenges, future outlook" template?
7. **Elegant variation:** Are synonyms cycling unnecessarily?
8. **Rule of three:** Are triads being used to pad thin observations?
9. **Em dash overuse:** Are dashes doing the work of commas and parentheses?
10. **Bold overuse:** Is every term bolded on first mention?

**The fix for all of these:** Write with specificity. Use plain words. Say "is" not "serves as." Cut any sentence that analyzes significance without adding information. Prefer short, direct constructions over balanced parallelisms. Let facts speak without editorial inflation.
