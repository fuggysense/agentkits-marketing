# Ideation Frameworks — Originality-First Content Creation

These frameworks generate ideas that are hard to copy because they start from what's uniquely yours, not what's trending.

## Framework 1: Collision Method

Combine two domains that only YOU sit at the intersection of.

**Process:**
1. List 3-5 domains you have real experience in (not surface-level interest)
2. List 3-5 content formats that are working in DIFFERENT niches (not yours)
3. Collide them: [your domain] × [unexpected format] = original concept

**Example:**
- Domain: AI marketing automation
- Format borrowed from: cooking content (mise en place, step-by-step assembly, "taste test" reveal)
- Collision: "Mise en place for ad campaigns" — show every element laid out before assembly, then the build, then the "taste test" (real metrics)

**Why it works:** The format is familiar (people understand cooking content structure) but the domain application is novel. A copycat would need your specific domain knowledge to pull it off authentically.

**Quality check:** If the collision feels forced, it is. Good collisions feel obvious in hindsight — "why hasn't anyone done this?"

## Framework 2: Proprietary Insight Extraction

Turn your real data, results, or experiences into content that literally cannot be replicated without your access.

**Process:**
1. What numbers do you have that nobody else does? (campaign results, revenue data, A/B test outcomes, customer conversations)
2. What counterintuitive thing did you learn from those numbers?
3. Build content around the insight, not the data

**Example:**
- Data: Ran 200+ Meta ad tests, discovered that ads with typos in the first draft outperformed "polished" versions 62% of the time
- Insight: Imperfection signals authenticity to the algorithm and audience
- Content: "I spent $50k proving that perfect ads lose" — show the actual dashboard, the numbers, the progression

**Why it works:** Someone can copy your conclusion, but they can't copy your proof. The proof IS the content. Without the real screenshots, real numbers, real progression — the copy is just a claim.

**Quality check:** If you could make this content without having done the work, it's not proprietary enough.

## Framework 3: Perspective Flip

Take a universally accepted "best practice" in your niche and argue the opposite — using evidence.

**Process:**
1. List 5 "everyone knows" rules in your space
2. For each: have you seen or experienced the opposite being true?
3. Build content around the contrarian take WITH receipts

**Example:**
- Best practice: "Post at optimal times for maximum reach"
- Your evidence: You posted at 2am accidentally, got 3x normal engagement
- Content: "The best time to post is when nobody else is posting" — with the actual analytics showing the 2am post vs your "optimized" posts

**Why it works:** Contrarian takes get algorithmic boost (high comment rate = engagement signal). But the receipts make it uncopyable — anyone can say "posting times don't matter" but only you have YOUR specific proof.

**Quality check:** If you don't have evidence for the contrarian take, don't fake it. The whole point is the proof.

---

## Framework 4: Format Translation

Take a content format that dominates one platform and translate it to a platform where it doesn't exist yet.

**Process:**
1. Identify a format crushing it on Platform A (e.g., Twitter/X thread format)
2. Check if that format exists on Platform B (e.g., TikTok, LinkedIn, YouTube Shorts)
3. If not → translate the structure while adapting to Platform B's native language

**Example:**
- Format: The "day in my life" vlog (YouTube) → applied to B2B SaaS marketing
- Translation: "Day in the life of a $10M ARR marketing team" — but shot in the casual, behind-the-scenes vlog style that B2B never uses
- Platform: LinkedIn video (where everything is polished and corporate)

**Why it works:** You get first-mover advantage on Platform B. The format is proven (reduces risk) but novel in context (triggers algorithmic novelty signal). Copycats on Platform B are copying YOU, not the original format creator.

**Quality check:** If the format appears in more than 5% of content in your niche on the target platform, it's saturated — you're copying, not translating. One or two examples don't count as saturated. Check the ratio, not mere existence.

## Framework 5: Compound Stacking

Build content that references and builds on your OWN previous content, creating a web that's impossible to enter mid-stream.

**Process:**
1. Create a foundational piece (the "episode 1")
2. Each subsequent piece references, builds on, or contradicts your previous work
3. Inside jokes, callbacks, evolving narratives — your audience builds shared context with you

**Example:**
- Week 1: "I'm going to build a $100k/month agency using only AI tools — here's the plan"
- Week 4: "Remember when I said cold email was dead? I was wrong — here's what happened"
- Week 8: Callback to Week 1 plan, show what changed, audience feels like they're ON the journey

**Why it works:** A copycat can steal any single piece. But they can't steal the journey. Your audience has context that new copycat audiences don't. The content compounds — each piece is better because of what came before.

**Quality check:** If each piece works entirely standalone with no reference to your body of work, you're not compound stacking — you're just making content.

## Framework 6: Research-Fueled Ideation

Use the `deep-research` skill or `researcher` agent to gather raw material BEFORE ideating. Instead of pulling only from your own head, you research what exists — then create what doesn't.

**When to use:** New niche entry, declining content performance, competitor evolution, or when you want data-backed originality instead of gut-feel originality.

**Process:**
1. Define research scope (pick 3-5 angles from the list below)
2. Spawn research agents (use `deep-research` for comprehensive, `researcher` for quick)
3. Review findings — mark what's saturated, what's white space, what's emerging
4. Feed findings into Frameworks 1-5 as raw material

**Research Angles:**

| Angle | What to look for | Feeds which framework |
|---|---|---|
| Competitor content audit | Formats, angles, engagement levels, posting frequency of top 5-10 competitors | Collision Method (avoid their formats), Perspective Flip (find practices to challenge) |
| Adjacent niche scan | Formats crushing it in 2-3 niches your audience doesn't follow | Collision Method (unexpected format sources), Format Translation |
| Platform gap analysis | What dominates Platform A but doesn't exist on Platform B | Format Translation (first-mover opportunities) |
| Audience discourse mining | Complaints, questions, debates in communities/forums/subreddits | Perspective Flip (real evidence for contrarian takes), Proprietary Insight |
| Trend lifecycle check | Where current trends sit on the originals→copies→saturation curve | All frameworks (timing intelligence — ride early, skip late) |

**Example:**
- Research finds: Every AI marketing creator on YouTube does "tool review" content. It's saturated. But on LinkedIn, nobody is doing behind-the-scenes BUILD content showing AI tools in action on real campaigns.
- Collision: Take the "build in public" format (indie hacker niche) × AI marketing execution (your domain) → show real campaign builds using AI tools with real metrics at the end
- Format Translation: This format lives on YouTube/Twitter but is absent on LinkedIn video
- Result: Original concept backed by research that confirms the white space actually exists

**Quality check:** Research should INFORM ideation, not REPLACE it. If your concept is just "do what the research says is trending" — you're trend-following, not moat-building. The research tells you where the GAPS are. Your creativity fills them.

**Anti-pattern:** Don't research-loop forever. Cap at 2-3 research rounds max. If you still can't find white space, the insight is that this niche is saturated — pivot to a different angle or lean harder on Layers 7-10 (voice, messaging, proprietary data, personal context) which are uncopyable regardless of format saturation.

## Combining Frameworks

The strongest concepts combine 2-3 frameworks:

| Combination | Result |
|-------------|--------|
| Collision + Proprietary | Novel format backed by real data — very hard to copy |
| Perspective Flip + Compound | Contrarian series that evolves — audience invested in the arc |
| Format Translation + Layering | First-mover on new platform with stacked visual identity |
| Proprietary + Compound | Data-driven journey — each piece builds on real results from the last |
| Research-Fueled + Collision | Research confirms white space exists, then you fill it with an original collision — data-backed originality |
| Research-Fueled + Perspective Flip | Research surfaces the "accepted practices" + community debates, giving you real evidence for contrarian takes |
| Research-Fueled + Format Translation | Research identifies which formats dominate Platform A but are absent on Platform B — first-mover with proof |

## Anti-Patterns (Ideas That Feel Original But Aren't)

| Trap | Why it fails | Fix |
|------|-------------|-----|
| "My take on [trending topic]" | Everyone has a take. Yours isn't special unless you have unique data. | Add proprietary insight or skip it. |
| "I tried [popular tool] and here's what happened" | 50 other creators posted the same review this week. | Only if you used it in a way nobody else did. |
| Aesthetic-only differentiation | Pretty ≠ original. If the idea is generic, nice visuals just make it expensive generic. | Ideation first, aesthetics second. |
| "Inspired by [viral creator]" | This is copying with extra steps. The algorithm knows. | Borrow the structural principle, not the format. |
| Trend-jacking with your logo | Putting your brand on a trend doesn't make it yours. | Either add 3+ original layers or skip the trend. |
