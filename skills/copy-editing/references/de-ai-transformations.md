# De-AI Transformations Reference

Before/after examples showing HOW to fix common AI writing patterns. Use during Sweep 8 when you catch a pattern but aren't sure how to rewrite it naturally.

Source: hardikpandya/stop-slop (MIT), adapted for this stack.

---

## Throat-Clearing → Direct

**Before:**
> Here's the thing about pricing strategy: most founders get it wrong. The truth is, your pricing communicates more than your marketing ever will.

**After:**
> Most founders underprice. Your pricing signals quality before your copy gets a chance to.

**What changed:** Removed "Here's the thing" and "The truth is." Led with the claim. Second sentence states the mechanism, not a grand reveal.

---

## Binary Contrast → Single Statement

**Before:**
> It's not about working harder. It's about working on the right things.

**After:**
> Work on the right things.

**What changed:** Killed the "not X, but Y" setup. The positive statement stands alone. Four words instead of fifteen.

---

## False Agency → Human Subject

**Before:**
> The data tells us that churn spikes after day 14. The market demands faster onboarding. This insight unlocks a retention strategy that could transform your metrics.

**After:**
> We found churn spikes after day 14. Customers expect faster onboarding. Knowing this, you can redesign the first two weeks to keep them.

**What changed:** Replaced "the data tells us," "the market demands," and "this insight unlocks" with human subjects (we, customers, you). Concrete action replaces vague "transform your metrics."

---

## Vague Declarative → Specific Claim

**Before:**
> The implications are significant. The stakes are high for any team that ignores this trend. The reasons are structural and deeply embedded in how the industry operates.

**After:**
> Teams that ignore this lose 20-30% of pipeline to competitors who adapted. The cause: buyers now self-educate through 8+ touchpoints before talking to sales, and most funnels still assume 3.

**What changed:** Named the implications, quantified the stakes, specified the structural reason. Every sentence now contains information you can act on.

---

## Dramatic Fragmentation → Complete Thought

**Before:**
> Community. That's it. That's the whole strategy.

**After:**
> The entire strategy is community, built through consistent small interactions, not a single launch event.

**What changed:** Completed the fragment into a sentence that adds context. The original sounds punchy but says nothing about what "community" means in practice.

---

## Performative Emphasis → Plain Statement

**Before:**
> Make no mistake: this will change how you think about content. Full stop. Let that sink in for a moment.

**After:**
> This changes how you plan content. Here's why.

**What changed:** Stripped "make no mistake," "full stop," and "let that sink in." These phrases instruct the reader to be impressed instead of giving them a reason to be.

---

## Meta-Commentary → Just Do It

**Before:**
> Let me walk you through why this matters. As we'll see, the three pillars of retention marketing are more interconnected than most teams realize.

**After:**
> Retention marketing has three pillars, and they compound. Fixing one without the others wastes budget.

**What changed:** Deleted "let me walk you through" and "as we'll see." The reader doesn't need narration about what they're about to read. State the point and move.

---

## Telling → Showing

**Before:**
> This is genuinely hard. This is what real marketing strategy actually looks like. It's messy, it's complicated, and it requires deep expertise.

**After:**
> You'll test six positioning variants, throw out five, and find the winner converts 3x better. The first draft takes a week. The winning one takes a month of iteration.

**What changed:** Replaced assertions about difficulty with a description of what the work looks like. The reader now understands the difficulty through specifics, not adjectives.

---

## Negative Listing → Direct Statement

**Before:**
> Not paid ads. Not SEO. Not even influencer partnerships. The real growth lever is product-led onboarding.

**After:**
> Product-led onboarding drives more growth than paid, SEO, or influencers for this stage.

**What changed:** Stated the winner first. Mentioned the alternatives as comparison, not as a dramatic runway of negations.

---

## Three-Item Default → Natural Count

**Before:**
> There are three key benefits: speed, simplicity, and scale.

**After:**
> Two things matter here: speed and simplicity. Scale comes later, after you've nailed the first two.

**What changed:** Broke the automatic three-item pattern. Separated the items by priority instead of listing them as equals. The result reflects actual strategic thinking instead of a pattern-matched list.
