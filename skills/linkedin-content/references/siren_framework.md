# The SIREN Framework — Deep Reference

> Derived from analysis of 35 LinkedIn posts across two high-performing Singapore-based creators (Kelvin Kuok, 9.6K followers; Michelle Koh, 5K followers). Engagement data from March 2026.

## Why SIREN Works (The Psychology)

LinkedIn's feed is attention-hostile. Users scroll past 90%+ of content. The posts that stop the scroll activate **emotional processing before cognitive processing** — the reader feels something before they think about it.

SIREN is designed to trigger this sequence: **Feel → Identify → Engage → Share.**

## The Unfindable Content Principle

The highest-performing posts share one meta-property: **the content cannot be found anywhere else on the internet.**

When someone writes about Reid Hoffman's LinkedIn origin story (49 likes), every detail is Google-able. The reader gains nothing they couldn't get from a Wikipedia article. There's zero reason to engage — the content has no scarcity.

When someone writes about their grandmother calling at 11pm to tell them to quit (244 likes), that story exists in exactly one place: this post. No search engine returns it. No other creator has it. The reader is encountering something genuinely new — a lived experience that was previously locked inside one person's memory.

**This is why the interview process is non-negotiable.** AI can restructure and polish, but it cannot invent unfindable content. The raw material must come from real conversations with real people about real moments only they have lived.

### The Unfindable Test

Before publishing, ask: "If someone googled the core idea of this post, would they find it?"

- **Yes** → The post will underperform. It's competing with every other version of that idea.
- **No, because the story/angle/experience is uniquely mine** → The post has scarcity value. People engage because this is their only chance to encounter it.

This principle also explains why tactical posts (frameworks, tips, how-tos) consistently underperform story posts. Tactics are findable — someone else has written a version. Personal stories are not.

## S — STAKES

### What It Is
Something concrete is at risk. Not hypothetical, not abstract — something the author could actually lose or has already lost.

### Psychology
**Loss aversion** (Kahneman & Tversky). Humans process potential loss 2x more intensely than equivalent gain. A post about "what I risked" hits harder than "what I gained."

### Examples from Real Posts

**High stakes (200+ likes):**
- Taking a 2nd leave of absence from NUS while grandmother disapproves (education, family approval, financial security all at risk)
- Closing a viral food business at its peak to sit by dying father's bed (income, identity, success — all sacrificed)

**Low/no stakes (50-70 likes):**
- "How should you brand yourself across multiple companies?" (nothing at risk — just advice)
- LinkedIn's origin story about Reid Hoffman (Reid already won, zero tension)

### How to Activate
Ask the client: "What could you lose by sharing this?" If the answer is "nothing," the post needs more. Push for:
- Financial risk ("I turned down a $X offer because...")
- Relationship risk ("My family/partner/mentor disagreed with...")
- Reputation risk ("I'm about to say something my industry won't like...")
- Identity risk ("This contradicts what I've been saying for years...")

### Intensity Scale
1. **Career/livelihood at stake** → highest engagement
2. **Important relationship at stake** → very high
3. **Reputation/credibility at stake** → high
4. **Comfort/convenience at stake** → moderate
5. **Nothing at stake** → will underperform

## I — IDENTITY

### What It Is
The post declares who the author IS — not what they did, not what they advise, but their fundamental identity.

### Psychology
**Self-concept theory** (Carl Rogers). People engage with mirrors. When a post declares an identity, readers unconsciously ask "Am I this person too?" That internal question drives comments. Identity posts turn passive readers into participants.

### Examples from Real Posts

**Strong identity declarations:**
- "They didn't choose entrepreneurship. They just couldn't imagine doing anything else. And neither can I." (244 likes)
- "Your mess is your message. Your rebuild is your relevance. Your survival is your stage." (151 likes)
- "I'm a living example of what happens when you show up consistently and let your work speak for you." (192 likes)

**Weak/absent identity:**
- LinkedIn origin story — entirely about Reid Hoffman, zero identity from the author (49 likes)
- "Here are my 3 answers about branding across companies" — advice-giving, not identity (72 likes)

### How to Activate
End the post with who the author IS, not what the reader should DO.

- "This is why I'm so passionate about X" ✓
- "Here are 3 tips for you" ✗
- "I just couldn't imagine doing anything else" ✓
- "Try these steps this week" ✗

### The Identity Spectrum
1. **"I am" statements** → strongest (I am someone who...)
2. **"I chose" statements** → strong (I chose this path because...)
3. **"I believe" statements** → moderate (I believe that...)
4. **"You should" statements** → weakest (advice without identity)

## R — REBELLION

### What It Is
The post challenges one accepted belief in the author's field — not for shock value, but because the author has lived the alternative and has proof it works.

### Psychology
**Reactance theory** (Brehm). When someone articulates what you've secretly felt but couldn't say, it creates cognitive liberation. The reader thinks "FINALLY someone said it." That relief is shareable.

Also: **Pattern interruption.** In a feed of "5 tips for..." posts, rebellion stops the scroll because the brain detects a threat to existing beliefs, which demands processing.

### Examples from Real Posts

**Effective rebellion (backed by proof):**
- "I don't follow morning routines. Never have. Probably never will." → Then proves productivity without them (96 likes)
- "Who told you I was suffering?" → Challenges the assumption entrepreneurship = pain (86 likes)
- "Nobody goes viral overnight and sustains a pipeline" → Challenges the viral growth fantasy (126 likes)

**Rebellion that works has 3 parts:**
1. State the common belief clearly
2. Share your personal experience that contradicts it
3. Show the result of your alternative approach

### How to Activate
Interview question: "What's a piece of common advice in your industry that you think is wrong?"

Then validate with: "What's your proof? What happened when you did it differently?"

**Empty rebellion (no proof) = hot take = backlash risk.**

## E — EMOTION

### What It Is
The first 2 lines trigger an emotional response before the reader has processed the content intellectually.

### Psychology
**Affect heuristic** (Slovic). Humans make snap judgments based on current emotional state. A post that makes you feel something gets engaged with before rational analysis kicks in. Front-load emotion, deliver the framework while the reader is emotionally open.

### The Emotion Hierarchy (strongest → weakest)

| Emotion | Example Hook | Typical Engagement |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Grief/Loss | "After my dad passed, I looked in the mirror..." | 150+ likes |
| Fear | "My grandmother called to tell me I was ruining my life" | 200+ likes |
| Pride | "22-year-old with no credentials earning a seat at the table" | 190+ likes |
| Relief | "Someone asked me 'who told you I was suffering?'" | 85+ likes |
| Curiosity | "I discovered LinkedIn's hidden algorithm" | 150+ likes |
| Interest | "How should you brand yourself across companies?" | 70 likes |

### How to Activate
Compare two openings for the same story:

- **Information-first (low engagement):** "How should you brand yourself if you run more than one company? Here are my 3 answers."
- **Emotion-first (high engagement):** "I had to let go of a client recently and it was a horrible experience. Here's what happened."

Always lead with the feeling. The information can come in paragraph 3.

### The 2-Line Test
Read only the first 2 lines of your draft. Do they make you feel:
- Tension? ✓
- Curiosity about a person? ✓
- Surprise? ✓
- Nothing? ✗ Rewrite.

## N — NAMING

### What It Is
Giving a label to something people felt but couldn't articulate. Creating a mental shortcut the reader will use forever — and credit you for.

### Psychology
**Labeling effect** (Tversky). Once something has a name, it becomes real, discussable, and shareable. "I feel weird about some friendships" is vague. "You have wrong people in your life" is actionable.

Also: **Gary Halbert's "naming the mechanism."** The best persuasion doesn't just promise a result — it names the thing that produces it.

### Examples from Real Posts

**Effective naming:**
- "Wrong people" vs "bad people" — created a new category people instantly recognized (76 likes, very high comment ratio)
- "The hidden algorithm is generosity" — named a pattern (152 likes)
- "Selling as leadership" — reframed an entire activity (recurring across Michelle's posts)

**Manufactured naming (less effective):**
- "SWIFT (Story-to-Warm-Intent Framework for Trust)" — feels like a backronym, not a discovery. Acronyms rarely feel organic.

### The Naming Test
A name works if:
1. The reader's first reaction is "oh, THAT'S what that is" (recognition)
2. They could explain it to someone else in one sentence (simplicity)
3. It feels discovered, not branded (authenticity)

### How to Activate
Interview question: "Have you noticed a pattern in your work that most people miss? What would you call it?"

Often the client won't have a name yet — your job is to listen for the insight and help them name it. The best names come from the client's own vocabulary, not your creativity.

## SIREN Scoring

For any draft, score each element 0-2:

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 0 | Element is absent |
| 1 | Element is present but weak |
| 2 | Element is strong and specific |

**Target: 7+ out of 10 for Tier 1 posts, 5+ for Tier 2, 3+ for Tier 3.**

| Total Score | Expected Performance |
|-------------|---------------------|
| 8-10 | Breakout potential (150+ likes) |
| 6-7 | Strong performance (80-150 likes) |
| 4-5 | Average (40-80 likes) |
| 0-3 | Will likely underperform (<40 likes) |

## SIREN for Other Channels

The 5 SIREN elements (Stakes, Identity, Rebellion, Emotion, Naming) generalize to storytelling broadly — they work for blog posts, email narratives, podcast intros, and video scripts. However, the **scoring benchmarks above are LinkedIn-calibrated** (based on LinkedIn engagement data from March 2026). Other channels may have different performance thresholds. When applying SIREN outside LinkedIn, use the elements as a writing framework but recalibrate scoring against channel-specific metrics.

Other skills can reference this framework without duplicating it — this file is the single source of truth for SIREN.
