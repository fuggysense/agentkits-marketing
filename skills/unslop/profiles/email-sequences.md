## Diagnostic / meta-commentary to never include

- Pre-email "diagnostic" sections assessing awareness level, trust level, emotional state, or market sophistication.
- References to copywriting "masters" by name: Halbert, Collier, Hopkins, Cialdini, Caples, Schwartz, Hormozi, Ogilvy, Ries & Trout. Do not cite them, attribute techniques to them, or mention their "principles" or "rules."
- "Sequence notes," "Sequence logic," "Why this works," "Implementation notes," or "Customization notes" sections appended after the emails.
- Markdown summary tables mapping each email to its "job," "mechanism," or "master."
- Meta-instructions telling the user to "personalize this," "swap in your real numbers," or "the more specific and real, the better these perform."
- Annotations about CTA strategy ("one CTA per email," "single CTA," "every email has exactly one action").
- Restating the principle that "specifics beat generalities" or any variation.

## Phrases to never use

- "Reply to this email" + "a real person reads these" / "I read every reply"
- "No hard feelings" / "no hard feelings either way"
- "Here's what..." / "Here's the thing" / "Here's exactly what..." as a transition into content
- "That's it." / "That's the whole [system/thing/point]"
- "Quick one" / "Quick note" / "Quick update" / "Quick question" / "Quick check-in" / "Quick stat" / "Quick math"
- "Genuinely" as a sincerity intensifier ("I genuinely want to know")
- "Seriously." as a standalone sentence
- "I'm not going to [pitch you / guilt-trip you / recap / pretend / manufacture pressure]"
- "Won't pretend" / "I'll be straight" / "Honest answer:" / "I'll be honest" / "Let me be direct"
- "Not a pitch" / "No pitch" / "Not trying to sell you" / "Not here to convince you"
- "Either way" as a closer ("Either way, good luck with...")
- "Closing the loop" / "Closing your file"
- "Takes about [specific time]" when fabricated for credibility
- "Short email." / "I'll keep this short" / "Not going to write you a long email"

## Sentence and paragraph structures to avoid

- The negative triplet: "Not [X]. Not [Y]. [Z]."
- The "This isn't [cliche]. This is [reframe]." pattern
- The ABC fork that segments readers into labeled buckets with separate CTAs: "(a) See potential here -> [link] (b) Decided it's not the right fit -> tell me (c) Haven't had time -> reply 'later'"
- The "If you're [Segment A]... If you're [Segment B]..." branching structure.
- Calling out what the email ISN'T doing as a rhetorical move ("No 'we miss you!' email," "Not a 'just checking in' email")

## Tonal patterns to avoid

- Performed anti-marketing: writing emails whose primary stance is "I'm not like other marketers." Do not build trust by constantly announcing what you're not doing.
- Hedged transparency on repeat: announcing honesty ("Honest answer:", "I'll be straight with you") more than once in a sequence. Real transparency doesn't need to announce itself.
- Explaining why every deadline or scarcity is "real" ("Not as a pressure tactic," "We're not manufacturing urgency"). If the scarcity is real, state the constraint. Don't also explain that you're not lying about it.
- The "if something's wrong, tell us" objection preempt used as a template line.
- Sardonic, self-aware, conspicuously "casual" voice applied identically regardless of brand, industry, or audience.

## Structural patterns to avoid

- The universal five-beat arc applied to every sequence: (1) value/story/no ask -> (2) proof/social proof -> (3) objection handling -> (4) urgency/deadline/scarcity -> (5) breakup/final email with loss aversion. Not every sequence needs all five beats or this order.
- A mandatory "breakup email" at the end of every sequence ("This is the last email I'll send").
- A P.S. on nearly every email used as a structural load-bearing element.
- Opening every first email with a personal anecdote or specific scenario.
- Opening later emails with "You probably [already know / feel / did]..."
- Every number being oddly precise for credibility ("11 minutes," "$87,000," "4,200 engineers"). When every number is hyper-specific, none of them feel specific.

## Formatting habits to avoid

- Em-dash density above ~5 per email.
- Parenthetical asides as the primary voice tool.
- Uniform formatting across every email in the sequence. Real writers vary their rhythm.

---

Instead of reaching for any of these defaults, vary your approach. Match voice to the actual brand. Match structure to the actual situation. If you catch yourself about to use any pattern listed above, stop and do something different.
